Section 01
What Is a Gene?

The basic vocabulary you need before anything else makes sense.

The Big Idea

Your body is built from instructions stored in your DNA. A gene is one specific instruction, like a recipe in a cookbook. Different versions of the same recipe are called alleles. You get one copy from mom, one from dad.

Example
Think of eye color. There is a gene for eye color (the recipe), but the gene comes in different versions (alleles): one version might code for brown, another for blue. You inherited one allele from each parent.
Gene
A segment of DNA that codes for a trait
Allele
A version of a gene (e.g., brown vs. blue)
Locus
The specific spot on a chromosome where a gene lives
Homozygous
Both alleles are the same (BB or bb)
Heterozygous
The two alleles are different (Bb)
Genotype
The alleles you carry (e.g., Bb)
Phenotype
What you actually look like (e.g., brown eyes)
Dominant
The allele that shows up even with just one copy (B)
Recessive
Only shows up when you have two copies (bb)
Section 02
Mendel's Laws

Two rules that predict how traits are passed from parents to offspring.

Law of Segregation

When your body makes egg or sperm cells, the two alleles for every gene split apart so each egg or sperm gets only one allele. This happens because the matching (homologous) chromosomes separate during meiosis I.

Example
If you are Bb for eye color, half your sperm or eggs carry B and the other half carry b. Each child gets one allele from you, chosen at random.

Law of Independent Assortment

Genes on different chromosomes get sorted into eggs and sperm independently of each other. Getting the "B" allele for one gene doesn't affect which allele you get for a gene on a different chromosome.

Example
If you are Bb (eye color) and Tt (tongue-rolling), the B allele is equally likely to end up with T or with t. The genes don't travel as a package because they sit on different chromosomes.

The Classic Crosses

The easiest way to predict offspring is a Punnett square. Here are the two crosses you will see over and over:

Monohybrid cross (one gene, both parents Aa):

Aa
AAAAa
aAaaa

Result: 3 dominant : 1 recessive phenotypic ratio.

Dihybrid cross (two genes, both parents AaBb):

Use a 4x4 Punnett square (16 boxes). The phenotypic ratio comes out 9:3:3:1, which confirms independent assortment.

Key Point
A testcross is when you cross a dominant-phenotype individual with a homozygous recessive (aa) to figure out whether the dominant individual is AA or Aa. If any recessive offspring appear, the parent must be Aa.
Section 03
Beyond Simple Dominance

Not every trait follows the neat dominant/recessive pattern.

Incomplete Dominance

The heterozygote is a blend of the two homozygotes.

Example
Red flower (RR) Ã— White flower (R'R') = Pink flower (RR'). The F2 ratio becomes 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white instead of 3:1.

Codominance

Both alleles show up fully and separately (no blending).

Example
Blood type AB. People with genotype IAIB have both A and B antigens on their red blood cells, not a mix.

ABO Blood Types (Multiple Alleles)

Three alleles exist in the population (IA, IB, and i), but each person only carries two. IA and IB are codominant with each other and both are dominant over i.

Epistasis

One gene overrides another gene entirely, changing the expected 9:3:3:1 ratio.

Example
In Labradors, the E gene controls whether pigment is deposited at all. Dogs with ee are yellow regardless of whether they carry the B (black) or b (chocolate) allele. The E gene is "epistatic" to the B gene.

Pleiotropy & Polygenic Traits

Pleiotropy: one gene affects many traits. Sickle cell allele impacts blood, spleen, heart, and brain.

Polygenic: many genes affect one trait, creating a smooth bell curve. Height and skin color are classic examples.

Section 04
Chromosomes & Sex Linkage

Why some traits are more common in one sex, and how genes that sit close together on the same chromosome get inherited as a package.

Sex Determination

In humans, females are XX and males are XY. The SRY gene on the Y chromosome triggers male development. No SRY = female development.

X-Linked Inheritance

Genes on the X chromosome follow special rules because males only have one X. If that single X carries a recessive allele, the male will show the trait (there is no second X to mask it). This is why conditions like color blindness and hemophilia are far more common in males.

Example
A carrier mom (XHXh) and a normal dad (XHY): half of their sons will be XhY (affected), half their daughters will be carriers. None of the daughters show the disease, but half carry it.
Key Point
X-inactivation: In every cell of a female mammal, one X chromosome is randomly shut off (forming a Barr body). This is why calico cats have patches of orange and black fur; different cells inactivated different X chromosomes.

Linkage & Recombination

Genes on the same chromosome tend to travel together (they are "linked"). They break Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment because they don't sort independently.

However, crossing over during meiosis I can swap pieces between homologous chromosomes, creating new allele combinations (recombinants).

Key Point
1% recombination = 1 centiMorgan (cM) = 1 map unit. The farther apart two genes are on a chromosome, the more often crossing over happens between them. Maximum recombination = 50%.
Section 05
DNA Structure & Replication

What DNA looks like and how it copies itself.

DNA Structure

DNA is a double helix: two strands twisted around each other, running in opposite directions (antiparallel). The outside is a sugar-phosphate backbone. The inside has paired bases held together by hydrogen bonds.

Base pairing rules (Chargaff's rules):

A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds)
G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds)

Because G-C has more hydrogen bonds, DNA with more G-C is harder to pull apart (higher melting temperature).

Memory Trick
Purines (A, G) have two rings. Pyrimidines (C, T, U) have one ring. A purine always pairs with a pyrimidine.

How DNA Copies Itself

Replication is semiconservative: each new double helix contains one old strand and one brand-new strand (shown by the Meselson-Stahl experiment).

New DNA is always built in the 5' â†’ 3' direction. This creates an asymmetry:

Leading strand: built continuously toward the replication fork.
Lagging strand: built in short pieces called Okazaki fragments, which are later glued together.

Section 06
From Gene to Protein

How the information in DNA becomes a working protein, in two major steps.

The Central Dogma

DNA â†’ transcription â†’ mRNA â†’ translation â†’ Protein

Information flows from DNA to RNA to protein. The first step (making an RNA copy) is transcription. The second step (reading the RNA to build a protein) is translation.

Step 1: Transcription (DNA â†’ mRNA)

RNA polymerase reads one strand of the DNA (the template strand, read 3'â†’5') and builds a messenger RNA (mRNA) copy in the 5' â†’ 3' direction. In eukaryotes, this happens in the nucleus.

Before the mRNA leaves the nucleus, it gets three modifications:

1. A 5' cap (protects it, helps ribosomes find it)
2. A poly-A tail on the 3' end (protects from degradation)
3. Splicing: introns (non-coding) are cut out, exons (coding) are kept

Memory Trick
Exons are Expressed. Introns stay Inside (the nucleus, in the trash). Alternative splicing lets one gene produce multiple different proteins by mixing and matching exons.

Step 2: Translation (mRNA â†’ Protein)

Ribosomes read the mRNA three letters at a time. Each group of three (a codon) specifies one amino acid. Transfer RNA (tRNA) molecules deliver the right amino acid by matching their anticodon to the mRNA codon.

Key numbers: 64 codons code for 20 amino acids (the code is redundant).

Start codon: AUG (methionine) sets the reading frame.

Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA (no amino acid; release factor ends translation).

Example
The mRNA sequence AUG-GCA-UAA would be read as: start (Met) - Ala - STOP. The protein would be just two amino acids long.
Section 07
Gene Regulation

Cells don't use every gene all the time. Here's how they turn genes on and off.

Prokaryotic Regulation: Operons

Bacteria group related genes together under one on/off switch called an operon. The two you need to know:

lac operon (inducible, for digesting lactose):

No lactose â†’ repressor blocks transcription (OFF)
Lactose present â†’ lactose removes the repressor (ON)
For maximum expression, glucose must also be absent (high cAMP activates CAP protein, which boosts transcription).

trp operon (repressible, for making tryptophan):

No tryptophan â†’ operon is ON (cell needs to make it)
Tryptophan abundant â†’ tryptophan activates the repressor, which shuts things down (OFF). Why make it if you already have it?

Eukaryotic Regulation (Multiple Levels)

Eukaryotes regulate genes at many stages. The quick version:

Chromatin level: Acetylation of histones = loosens DNA = gene ON. DNA methylation = tightens DNA = gene OFF.
Transcription level: Transcription factors, enhancers, and silencers control whether RNA polymerase starts.
After transcription: Alternative splicing, mRNA stability, and small RNAs (miRNA) fine-tune the message.
After translation: Proteins can be modified, activated, or tagged for destruction.

Section 08
Mutations & DNA Repair

What happens when the DNA instructions get a typo.

Point Mutations (Single Base Changes)

TypeWhat HappensEffect
SilentBase changes but the amino acid stays the sameNone (thanks to code redundancy)
MissenseBase changes, different amino acid insertedMay or may not affect protein function
NonsenseBase change creates a premature STOP codonTruncated protein, usually broken

Frameshift Mutations

If you insert or delete nucleotides in a number that isn't a multiple of 3, you shift the entire reading frame. Every codon downstream is now wrong. These are almost always devastating.

Example
Original: AUG-GCA-UUC-...
Delete one "G": AUG-CAU-UC... Everything after the deletion is misread.

DNA Repair

Cells have multiple proofreading and repair systems:

Proofreading: DNA Pol III catches and fixes errors as it builds.
Mismatch repair: Fixes base-pairing mistakes after replication.
Nucleotide excision repair (NER): Cuts out big damage like UV-caused thymine dimers. A defect here causes xeroderma pigmentosum (extreme sun sensitivity).
Base excision repair: Fixes individual damaged bases.

Section 09
Chromosome Changes

What happens when whole chromosomes (or large pieces) go wrong.

Aneuploidy

When chromosomes fail to separate properly during meiosis (nondisjunction), you end up with cells that have too many or too few chromosomes.

Structural Changes

Pieces of chromosomes can also be rearranged:

Deletion: a piece is lost (e.g., Cri-du-chat syndrome).
Duplication: a piece is copied twice.
Inversion: a piece is flipped 180°.
Translocation: a piece moves to a completely different chromosome.

Section 10
Biotechnology Basics

The toolkit scientists use to cut, copy, and edit DNA.

Restriction Enzymes & Cloning

Restriction enzymes are molecular scissors that cut DNA at specific sequences. They can produce "sticky ends" that let you paste DNA from different sources together. DNA ligase seals the join.

To clone a gene: cut both the gene and a plasmid (small circular DNA) with the same restriction enzyme, mix them so the gene inserts into the plasmid, then put the plasmid into bacteria. The bacteria copy the gene every time they divide.

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

PCR makes millions of copies of a specific DNA sequence in a test tube. Three steps repeat in cycles:

1. Denature (~95 °C): Heat separates the two DNA strands.
2. Anneal (~55 °C): Short primers bind to the target region.
3. Extend (~72 °C): Taq polymerase (heat-stable enzyme) builds new strands.

After n cycles you have 2n copies. 30 cycles = over a billion copies.

Gel Electrophoresis

Separates DNA fragments by size. DNA is negatively charged, so it moves toward the positive end of the gel. Smaller pieces move faster and farther.

Memory Trick
SNoW DRoP: Southern blot = DNA, Northern blot = RNA, Western blot = Protein.

CRISPR-Cas9

A gene-editing system borrowed from bacteria. A guide RNA leads the Cas9 protein to a precise spot in the genome, where Cas9 cuts both DNA strands. The cell's repair machinery can then knock out a gene or insert a new one.

Section 11
Population Genetics

How allele frequencies change (or don't) in a population over time.

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

If nothing is disturbing a population, allele frequencies stay the same generation after generation. The five conditions required for this (none of which are ever perfectly true in nature):

1. No mutation
2. Random mating
3. No natural selection
4. Huge population (no genetic drift)
5. No migration (gene flow)

The equations:

p + q = 1 (allele frequencies, where p = dominant, q = recessive)
p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies: AA + Aa + aa)

How to Solve a Problem
"1 in 2,500 people has disorder X (recessive)." â†’ q² = 1/2500 = 0.0004 â†’ q = 0.02 â†’ p = 0.98 â†’ carrier frequency (2pq) = 2(0.98)(0.02) = ~3.9%.

Forces That Change Allele Frequencies

Mutation
Creates new alleles; the raw material for evolution
Selection
Organisms with favorable traits survive and reproduce more; the only force that is adaptive
Genetic Drift
Random changes in allele frequency, strongest in small populations (bottleneck effect, founder effect)
Gene Flow
Migration moves alleles between populations, making them more similar
