Mendelian Genetics

Key Terminology

  • Gene: a unit of heredity; a segment of DNA encoding a functional product
  • Allele: an alternative form of a gene at a given locus
  • Locus: the specific physical position of a gene on a chromosome
  • Homozygous: two identical alleles (AA or aa)
  • Heterozygous: two different alleles (Aa)
  • Genotype: the genetic makeup (e.g., Aa)
  • Phenotype: the observable trait (e.g., purple flowers)
  • Dominant: allele expressed in the heterozygote. Recessive: masked in the heterozygote, expressed only when homozygous.

Mendel's Laws

  • Law of Segregation: during gamete formation, the two alleles for a gene separate so each gamete carries only one allele. This corresponds to the separation of homologous chromosomes in meiosis I.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different (non-homologous) chromosomes sort independently during gamete formation. This corresponds to the random orientation of homologous pairs at the metaphase plate in meiosis I.

Monohybrid Cross

Cross between two individuals heterozygous for one gene: Aa x Aa

  • Genotypic ratio: 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa
  • Phenotypic ratio: 3 dominant : 1 recessive

A testcross crosses an individual of unknown genotype (dominant phenotype) with a homozygous recessive (aa) to determine if it is AA or Aa.

Dihybrid Cross

Cross between two individuals heterozygous for two genes: AaBb x AaBb

  • Phenotypic ratio: 9:3:3:1
  • 9 A_B_ : 3 A_bb : 3 aaB_ : 1 aabb
  • This 9:3:3:1 ratio confirms independent assortment
Use the "forked-line method" or a 4x4 Punnett square (16 boxes) for dihybrid crosses.

Probability Rules

  • Multiplication rule (AND): probability of two independent events both occurring = product of their individual probabilities
  • Addition rule (OR): probability of one event OR another occurring = sum of their individual probabilities (for mutually exclusive events)
  • Example: Aa x Aa. P(Aa offspring) = P(A from dad AND a from mom) + P(a from dad AND A from mom) = (1/2)(1/2) + (1/2)(1/2) = 1/2

Chi-Square Goodness of Fit

Tests whether observed data fit an expected genetic ratio.

χ² = Σ (observed - expected)² / expected

  • Degrees of freedom = number of classes - 1
  • Compare calculated χ² to critical value at p = 0.05
  • If χ² < critical value: fail to reject the null hypothesis (data fit the expected ratio)
  • If χ² > critical value: reject the null hypothesis (data do not fit)

Extensions of Mendelism

Incomplete Dominance

The heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype between the two homozygotes. Neither allele is fully dominant.

  • Example: red (RR) x white (R'R') snapdragons = pink (RR') F1
  • F2 ratio: 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white

Codominance

Both alleles are fully expressed in the heterozygote (not blended).

  • Example: ABO blood types. IAIB individuals express both A and B antigens on red blood cells (type AB)
  • Sickle cell heterozygotes (HbA HbS) produce both normal and sickle hemoglobin

IA and IB are codominant to each other; both are dominant over i.

Epistasis

One gene masks or modifies the expression of a different gene. Modifies the 9:3:3:1 dihybrid ratio.

  • Recessive epistasis (9:3:4): e.g., Labrador coat color. The ee genotype at the E locus makes the dog yellow regardless of B locus genotype
  • Duplicate recessive epistasis (9:7): two genes required; homozygous recessive at either locus blocks expression
  • Complementary gene interaction (9:7), duplicate dominant epistasis (15:1): recognize these modified ratios

Pleiotropy & Polygenic Inheritance

  • Pleiotropy: one gene affects multiple phenotypic traits. Example: sickle cell allele affects blood, spleen, brain, heart.
  • Polygenic inheritance: multiple genes contribute to a single trait, producing a continuous distribution. Examples: skin color, height, weight.
  • Polygenic traits often show a bell curve distribution and are strongly influenced by environment.

Other Extensions

  • Lethal alleles: homozygosity for certain alleles causes death (often in utero). Modifies Mendelian ratios (e.g., 2:1 instead of 3:1). Example: yellow mice (AYAY is lethal).
  • Penetrance: the percentage of individuals with a given genotype who show the expected phenotype. Can be complete or incomplete.
  • Expressivity: the degree to which a genotype is phenotypically expressed. Can be variable.
  • Environmental effects: phenotype = genotype + environment. Example: temperature-sensitive coat color in Siamese cats.

Chromosome Theory & Linkage

Chromosome Theory of Inheritance

  • Genes are located on chromosomes, which are the physical basis of Mendel's laws
  • Chromosomes exist in homologous pairs in diploid organisms
  • Segregation of alleles = separation of homologs during meiosis I
  • Independent assortment = random orientation of different homologous pairs at the metaphase plate

Sex Determination

  • Humans: XX = female, XY = male
  • The SRY gene on the Y chromosome triggers male development
  • Other systems: ZW (birds, butterflies: ZZ = male, ZW = female), XO (grasshoppers), haplodiploidy (bees: haploid males, diploid females)

X-Linked Inheritance

  • Males are hemizygous for X-linked genes (only one copy)
  • X-linked recessive traits appear more frequently in males because they need only one copy of the recessive allele
  • An affected father passes the allele to all daughters (carriers) but no sons
  • A carrier mother passes the allele to 1/2 of sons (affected) and 1/2 of daughters (carriers)
  • Examples: hemophilia, red-green color blindness, Duchenne muscular dystrophy

X-Inactivation

  • In female mammals, one X chromosome is randomly inactivated in each cell during early development, forming a Barr body
  • Ensures dosage compensation between XX females and XY males
  • Results in mosaic expression of X-linked genes in heterozygous females
  • Example: calico cats (patches of orange and black fur)

Genetic Linkage

  • Genes on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together (linked genes)
  • Linked genes do NOT assort independently; they violate Mendel's second law
  • A testcross with a dihybrid reveals linkage when offspring ratios deviate significantly from 1:1:1:1
  • Parental (non-recombinant) classes are more frequent; recombinant classes are less frequent

Recombination & Gene Mapping

  • Crossing over during meiosis I can separate linked genes, producing recombinant gametes
  • Recombination frequency (RF) = (recombinant offspring / total offspring) x 100%
  • RF is a measure of map distance: 1% recombination = 1 centiMorgan (cM) = 1 map unit
  • Maximum RF = 50% (genes appear unlinked because they are far apart on the same chromosome or on different chromosomes)
  • Three-point testcross: used to map three linked genes simultaneously. The least frequent class = double crossover, and comparing it to parentals reveals the gene order.

DNA Structure & Replication

DNA Structure

  • Double helix of two antiparallel polynucleotide strands (Watson & Crick, 1953)
  • Each nucleotide = deoxyribose sugar + phosphate group + nitrogenous base
  • Sugar-phosphate backbone on the outside; bases on the inside
  • Strands run antiparallel: one 5'→3', the other 3'→5'
  • Strands held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs

Base Pairing Rules (Chargaff's Rules)

  • A = T (2 hydrogen bonds)
  • G ≡ C (3 hydrogen bonds)
  • Purines (A, G) always pair with pyrimidines (T, C)
  • Therefore: %A = %T and %G = %C in double-stranded DNA
  • Higher G-C content = higher melting temperature (more H-bonds)
"Pure As Gold" = Purines are Adenine and Guanine. "CUT the Py" = Pyrimidines are Cytosine, Uracil (RNA), Thymine.

DNA Replication: Overview

  • Semiconservative: each daughter molecule contains one original strand and one newly synthesized strand (proved by Meselson-Stahl experiment)
  • Proceeds bidirectionally from origins of replication
  • New strand synthesized 5' → 3' only
  • Leading strand: synthesized continuously toward the replication fork
  • Lagging strand: synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments, away from the fork

Transcription & Translation

The Central Dogma

DNA → (transcription) → RNA → (translation) → Protein

Information flows from DNA to RNA to protein. Reverse transcriptase (retroviruses) can copy RNA back to DNA, but protein sequence is never reverse-translated.

Transcription: DNA → mRNA

  • RNA polymerase reads the template strand (3'→5') and synthesizes mRNA in the 5' → 3' direction
  • The mRNA sequence is identical to the coding strand (non-template), except U replaces T
  • Promoter: DNA sequence where RNA polymerase binds to start transcription
  • Terminator: signal that ends transcription
  • In prokaryotes: transcription and translation are coupled (occur simultaneously)
  • In eukaryotes: transcription in the nucleus, translation in the cytoplasm

Eukaryotic mRNA Processing

  • 5' cap: modified guanine added to the 5' end; protects mRNA, aids ribosome binding
  • 3' poly-A tail: ~200 adenines added to the 3' end; protects from degradation, aids export from nucleus
  • RNA splicing: introns are removed, exons are joined together by the spliceosome
  • Alternative splicing: different combinations of exons produce different mRNAs (and proteins) from the same gene
"Exons are Expressed, Introns are In the trash."

The Genetic Code

  • Triplet code: three nucleotides (a codon) specify one amino acid
  • 64 codons total (4³), coding for 20 amino acids
  • Code is degenerate (redundant): most amino acids are specified by more than one codon (usually the 3rd position varies)
  • Code is universal (nearly all organisms use the same code, with rare exceptions)
  • Code is non-overlapping and read in a specific reading frame
  • Start codon: AUG (methionine; also sets reading frame)
  • Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA
"U Are Annoying, U Are Gone, U Go Away" for UAA, UAG, UGA stop codons.

Translation: mRNA → Protein

  • Occurs on ribosomes (in cytoplasm or on rough ER)
  • tRNA molecules carry amino acids; each has an anticodon complementary to the mRNA codon
  • Ribosome has three sites: A (aminoacyl/incoming), P (peptidyl/growing chain), E (exit)
  • Initiation: small subunit binds mRNA at AUG, initiator tRNA enters P site, large subunit joins
  • Elongation: tRNA enters A site, peptide bond forms (by peptidyl transferase/ribosomal RNA), ribosome translocates
  • Termination: stop codon in A site, release factor binds, polypeptide is released

Gene Regulation

Prokaryotic Gene Regulation: The Operon Model

  • An operon is a cluster of co-regulated genes under one promoter, transcribed as a single mRNA (polycistronic)
  • Components: promoter, operator (repressor binding site), structural genes, regulatory gene (encodes repressor)

lac Operon (Inducible)

  • Encodes enzymes for lactose metabolism
  • No lactose present: repressor binds operator, blocks transcription (OFF)
  • Lactose present: allolactose (inducer) binds repressor, changes its shape, repressor falls off operator, transcription proceeds (ON)
  • Also regulated by CAP-cAMP: when glucose is absent, cAMP is high, CAP-cAMP binds upstream and enhances transcription. When glucose is present, cAMP is low, no enhancement (catabolite repression).
The lac operon needs BOTH conditions: lactose present AND glucose absent for maximum expression.

trp Operon (Repressible)

  • Encodes enzymes for tryptophan biosynthesis
  • Trp absent: repressor is inactive, transcription is ON (cell makes tryptophan)
  • Trp present: tryptophan acts as a corepressor, binds the repressor, activates it so it binds the operator and blocks transcription (OFF)
  • Logic: why make tryptophan if it is already available?

Eukaryotic Gene Regulation

Regulation occurs at multiple levels:

  • Chromatin remodeling: acetylation of histones loosens chromatin (promotes transcription); methylation of DNA typically silences genes
  • Transcriptional: transcription factors, enhancers, silencers, mediator complexes
  • Post-transcriptional: alternative splicing, mRNA stability, miRNA (degrades mRNA or blocks translation)
  • Translational: initiation factor regulation
  • Post-translational: protein folding, modification (phosphorylation, ubiquitination), degradation

Mutation & DNA Repair

Types of Point Mutations

TypeChangeEffect
SilentBase change, same amino acidNo effect on protein (due to code degeneracy)
MissenseBase change, different amino acidMay or may not affect function (depends on location)
NonsenseBase change creates a stop codonTruncated protein, usually nonfunctional

Frameshift Mutations

  • Caused by insertions or deletions (indels) of nucleotides that are NOT multiples of three
  • Shifts the entire reading frame downstream of the mutation
  • Usually produces a completely nonfunctional protein
  • Insertions or deletions of 3 nucleotides (or multiples of 3) add or remove amino acids but do not cause a frameshift

Mutagens

  • Chemical mutagens: base analogs (5-bromouracil), alkylating agents, deaminating agents, intercalating agents (cause frameshifts)
  • Physical mutagens: UV light (causes thymine dimers), X-rays and gamma rays (cause breaks in DNA)
  • Biological mutagens: transposons (jumping genes)
  • Spontaneous mutations: replication errors, depurination, deamination

DNA Repair Mechanisms

  • Proofreading: DNA polymerase 3'→5' exonuclease activity corrects errors during replication
  • Mismatch repair: enzymes detect and fix base mismatches after replication
  • Nucleotide excision repair (NER): removes bulky lesions like thymine dimers. Defective in xeroderma pigmentosum.
  • Base excision repair (BER): removes individual damaged bases via glycosylase
  • SOS repair: error-prone emergency repair in prokaryotes when damage is extensive

Chromosome Variation

Changes in Chromosome Number

  • Euploidy: changes in complete sets of chromosomes
  • Polyploidy: 3n (triploid), 4n (tetraploid), etc. Common in plants, often from nondisjunction. Important in plant speciation and agriculture.
  • Aneuploidy: gain or loss of individual chromosomes, usually from nondisjunction during meiosis
  • Monosomy (2n - 1): one chromosome missing. Trisomy (2n + 1): one extra chromosome.

Changes in Chromosome Structure

  • Deletion: loss of a chromosome segment. Example: Cri-du-chat (5p deletion).
  • Duplication: a segment is copied and present twice. Can lead to gene dosage effects.
  • Inversion: a segment is flipped 180° within the chromosome. Paracentric (does not include centromere) vs. pericentric (includes centromere).
  • Translocation: a segment moves to a non-homologous chromosome. Reciprocal: exchange between two chromosomes. Robertsonian: two acrocentric chromosomes fuse at centromeres.

Recombinant DNA & Biotechnology

Restriction Enzymes

  • Bacterial enzymes that cut DNA at specific palindromic recognition sequences
  • Produce sticky ends (overhanging single-stranded) or blunt ends
  • Sticky ends are preferred for cloning because they can base-pair with complementary sticky ends from another source
  • DNA ligase seals fragments together to create recombinant DNA

Cloning Vectors & Gene Cloning

  • A vector carries foreign DNA into a host cell. Common vectors: plasmids, bacteriophages, BACs, YACs.
  • Plasmid vectors typically contain: origin of replication, selectable marker (e.g., antibiotic resistance gene), multiple cloning site
  • Steps: cut vector and insert DNA with same restriction enzyme, ligate together, transform into bacteria, select for transformants (e.g., on ampicillin plates)
  • cDNA library: made from mRNA using reverse transcriptase; represents only expressed genes
  • Genomic library: contains fragments of the entire genome

PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

  • Amplifies a specific DNA sequence in vitro (millions of copies from a tiny sample)
  • Requires: template DNA, two primers, Taq polymerase (thermostable), dNTPs
  • Three steps per cycle: Denaturation (~95°C): strands separate. Annealing (~55°C): primers bind. Extension (~72°C): Taq extends.
  • n cycles = 2n copies of the target sequence
  • Applications: forensics, diagnostics, cloning, sequencing

Gel Electrophoresis

  • Separates DNA, RNA, or protein fragments by size
  • DNA is negatively charged (phosphate groups) and migrates toward the positive electrode (anode)
  • Smaller fragments move faster (travel farther through the gel)
  • Agarose gels for DNA; polyacrylamide gels (PAGE) for proteins or small DNA
  • Southern blot = DNA, Northern blot = RNA, Western blot = protein
"SNoW DRoP" = Southern/Northern/Western detect DNA/RNA/Protein.

CRISPR-Cas9

  • A powerful gene editing system adapted from bacterial immune defense
  • Guide RNA (gRNA) directs the Cas9 nuclease to a specific complementary DNA sequence
  • Cas9 makes a double-strand break at the target site
  • Cell repair (NHEJ or HDR) can knock out a gene or insert a new sequence
  • Applications: gene therapy, agriculture, disease models, functional genomics

Other Key Techniques

  • Sanger sequencing: uses ddNTPs (chain terminators) to determine DNA sequence
  • DNA fingerprinting: uses STRs (short tandem repeats) or RFLPs to identify individuals
  • Microarrays: measure expression of thousands of genes simultaneously
  • Transgenic organisms: contain foreign DNA from another species. Example: Bt crops (contain bacterial toxin gene for pest resistance).
  • Gene therapy: introducing a functional gene to correct a genetic disorder

Genomics & Epigenetics

Genomics

  • Genome: the complete set of DNA in an organism
  • Human genome: ~3.2 billion bp, ~20,000-25,000 protein-coding genes, only ~1.5% codes for protein
  • Much of the genome is non-coding: introns, repetitive sequences, regulatory sequences, transposons
  • Comparative genomics: comparing genomes across species reveals conserved (functionally important) regions
  • Proteomics: study of the full set of proteins expressed by a cell or organism

Epigenetics

  • Heritable changes in gene expression that do NOT involve changes to the DNA sequence itself
  • DNA methylation: addition of methyl groups to cytosine (usually at CpG sites); typically silences gene expression
  • Histone modification: acetylation (loosens chromatin, promotes transcription), methylation (can activate or silence depending on context), phosphorylation
  • Genomic imprinting: certain genes are expressed only from one parent's allele (the other is silenced by methylation). Example: Prader-Willi and Angelman syndromes result from imprinting defects on chromosome 15.

Transposable Elements

  • "Jumping genes" discovered by Barbara McClintock in maize
  • Insertion sequences (IS): simplest transposons in prokaryotes; encode only transposase
  • Composite transposons: carry additional genes (e.g., antibiotic resistance) flanked by IS elements
  • Retrotransposons (eukaryotes): transpose via an RNA intermediate using reverse transcriptase (copy-and-paste mechanism)
  • Make up a large fraction of the human genome (~45%)

Population & Evolutionary Genetics

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

A population is in genetic equilibrium (allele frequencies do NOT change) if ALL five conditions are met:

  • No mutation
  • Random mating
  • No natural selection
  • Infinitely large population (no genetic drift)
  • No gene flow (migration)

Hardy-Weinberg equations for two alleles (p and q):

p + q = 1 (allele frequencies)

p² + 2pq + q² = 1 (genotype frequencies)

Where p² = frequency of AA, 2pq = frequency of Aa, q² = frequency of aa.

Solving H-W Problems

  • Usually, you are given the frequency of the recessive phenotype (q²) as your entry point
  • Take the square root of q² to find q
  • Subtract from 1 to find p (since p + q = 1)
  • Calculate carrier frequency: 2pq
  • Example: if 1 in 10,000 individuals has a recessive disorder, q² = 0.0001, q = 0.01, p = 0.99, carrier frequency = 2(0.99)(0.01) = 0.0198 or about 2%

Forces of Evolution

  • Mutation: ultimate source of new alleles; raw material for evolution
  • Natural selection: differential survival and reproduction based on phenotype. Only force that is adaptive (produces adaptation).
  • Genetic drift: random changes in allele frequency, especially powerful in small populations. Includes bottleneck effect and founder effect.
  • Gene flow: movement of alleles between populations (via migration). Tends to reduce differences between populations.
  • Non-random mating: assortative mating, inbreeding. Changes genotype frequencies (increases homozygosity) but does not change allele frequencies by itself.

Types of Natural Selection

  • Directional selection: favors one extreme phenotype (shifts the curve)
  • Stabilizing selection: favors the intermediate phenotype (narrows the curve). Example: human birth weight.
  • Disruptive selection: favors both extremes over the intermediate (splits the curve)
  • Balancing selection: maintains multiple alleles. Includes heterozygote advantage (e.g., sickle cell carriers are resistant to malaria) and frequency-dependent selection.

Inbreeding & Genetic Drift

  • Inbreeding: mating between relatives. Increases homozygosity, increases expression of deleterious recessive alleles. Measured by the inbreeding coefficient (F).
  • Bottleneck effect: drastic reduction in population size reduces genetic diversity
  • Founder effect: small group establishes a new population with a subset of alleles from the original population
